Key Signals
Strength — Program Outcomes

62% of FY25 case closures resulted in family reunification, up from 34% in FY24 and the best rate in five years. This is the single strongest program effectiveness signal in the dataset.

Strength — Fundraising Momentum

Active donors jumped 157% (108→278) in FY25, driving a 15% increase in donor giving to $149K. Cash giving alone surged 58% to $107K. Total program revenue (including government grants) reached $638K — against total expenses of $655K, producing a -$17K operating deficit. Revenue growth is strong; the FY26 priority is holding expense growth in check while sustaining fundraising momentum.

Strength — Volunteer Diversity

Non-white volunteers grew from 39% (FY21) to 52% (FY25), substantially exceeding the national CASA average of 25–30% and tracking toward demographic alignment with the children CASA serves.

Strength — Black/AA Goal Exceeded ✓

Black/African American advocates now represent 21% of the volunteer corps — exceeding the FY26 strategic goal of 20%. This is the only diversity metric to cross its target line, representing a 13-point gain from 8% in FY21. The recruitment strategy behind this gain should be documented and applied to the Hispanic/Latino and male advocate pipelines.

Watch — ~23% Annual Attrition Rate

Volunteers declined 5.5% from the FY24 peak of 128 to 121 in FY25. With 23 new recruits and a net loss of 7, approximately 30 volunteers departed — a ~23% annual attrition rate. At the current pace, the corps will continue to contract. The program needs to recruit ~30 per year to hold steady, not 20.Set a FY26 recruitment target of at least 30 new advocates (not 20) to break even on attrition. Track the monthly sworn-in count at every board meeting as the leading indicator of program capacity.

Watch — Donor Retention Risk

57% of FY25 donors (159 of 278) are giving for the first time. National first-year donor retention is ~19%, meaning ~130 could lapse without active stewardship — the most urgent FY26 fundraising risk.Assign each of the 71 multi-year donors a named board contact for a personal outreach call before May 30 — this cohort is the highest-return stewardship investment available right now.

Risk — Government Grant Concentration

Total program revenue in FY25 is $638K — against total expenses of $655K, for a -$17K operating deficit. Of that revenue, $499K (78%) comes from government grants; VOCA ($147K) and NJ State Pass-Thru ($230K) alone represent $377K, nearly 60% of the entire budget. A 20% cut to either stream would create a larger gap than losing every single major donor — and with the program already running a deficit, any revenue reduction hits harder than it would from a surplus position. The FY27 budget must model federal reduction scenarios before they become a crisis.Ask the Executive Director to brief the board on VOCA and NJ State Pass-Thru renewal timelines this quarter — and begin building a local foundation prospecting list of at least 5 targets to partially offset a potential federal reduction in FY27.

Risk — Donor Cash Concentration

Within the $107K raised from donors, 4 cash donors gave $5K+, representing 20% of all cash revenue ($21K). Two in-kind donors gave $5K+. This concentration creates vulnerability if major donors lapse — though the larger budget risk remains government grant dependency above.Initiate individual cultivation plans for the top 4 cash donors this quarter; simultaneously identify 3–5 mid-level prospects ($1K–$5K capacity) so donor revenue is not dependent on any single relationship.

Strength — Total Program Investment Growing

Total cost per child served reached $3,922 in FY25 (total expenses $655K ÷ 167 children served) — a meaningful benchmark for funders and court stakeholders. Revenue per child is $3,821, producing a -$99 net gap per child in FY25. This full-picture investment figure is the most compelling metric for grant applications and board reports.

Watch — 74 Children Eligible but Unserved

Of the 241 children in Middlesex County with an active out-of-home placement, only 167 have an assigned CASA advocate — leaving 74 children (31%) without one. This is the single most consequential gap in Pillar 1: every unserved child is navigating the court system without an independent adult advocate in their corner. Closing this gap is directly gated by volunteer recruitment — each new advocate sworn in reaches one additional child.Present the raw unserved count (74 children, not just the percentage) at every board meeting alongside the monthly sworn-in number — making the gap visible as a headcount, not a statistic, shifts it from a program metric to a moral imperative.

Risk — Aged-Out Rate Spiked to 12% in FY25

Six children aged out of foster care at 21 without family permanency in FY25 — 12% of all closures. This is the highest rate in the five-year dataset by a wide margin: the rate was 0% in FY23 and 1.5% in FY24. Each child who ages out represents a failure of the permanency system — transitioning to adulthood without a stable family. CASA should conduct an immediate proactive review of all children currently aged 17–18 on the active caseload to identify permanency barriers before they reach 21.Produce a list of all children currently aged 17–18 on the active caseload at the next Program Coordinator meeting — prioritize permanency planning for this cohort before June and report back to the board on the number identified and actions taken.

Program Quality & Impact — Summary & Insights

This pillar tracks CASA's core mission: delivering excellence in child advocacy and achieving measurable outcomes. The active caseload averaged 123 children per month (Jul 2025–Feb 2026), with strong case closure outcomes including a 5-year-high 62% reunification rate in FY25. The data below covers caseload census, demographics, petition types, geographic reach, and case outcomes — all key indicators of program quality and impact.

Watch — rising disparity

Black/African American children increased from 34 to 42 over 8 months (+24%), now the second-largest racial group at 33% of the census. White representation declined from 31% to 28% over the same period. This growing disparity warrants review of referral and intake patterns.Ask the court liaison to pull a referral-source breakdown by race at the next monthly meeting — the goal is to determine whether the shift reflects changes in court referral patterns or a broader placement policy change before it becomes a compliance question.

Trend — infant surge

Infants (0–11 months) rose from 8 to 14 — a 75% increase — the steepest growth of any age band. This signals a need to assess infant-specific placement capacity, foster parent training for young children, and early intervention service availability.

Trend — guardianship growth

FG (guardianship) petitions grew from 14 to 22 (+57%), suggesting more children are moving toward permanent family placements. This is a positive permanency indicator and may reflect successful casework or policy emphasis on kinship care.

Flag — FN petition decline

FN (standard foster care) petitions fell from 89 to 77 (-13%) while total census stayed relatively stable. Monitor whether this reflects genuine permanency progress or a shift in petition classification practices.Ask intake staff to flag any petition reclassifications at the next team meeting; if the decline is genuine permanency progress, document it explicitly in the FY26 board report — it is a strong outcome narrative worth quantifying.

Stable — gender balance

The caseload has maintained a near-equal gender split throughout (51% female, 49% male in July; shifting to 46% female, 54% male by February). The gradual male-skew trend is worth tracking across future months.

Stable — Asian representation

Asian children have remained fixed at exactly 8 throughout all 8 months — an unusual pattern that may reflect a data ceiling, classification boundary, or genuine demographic stability worth confirming with intake staff.

Watch — eligible coverage gap

CASA served 73–79% of eligible children (per bi-monthly census snapshots) against the FY26 goal of 80%. This is the single most strategically important metric in Pillar 1: the gap represents approximately 12–17 eligible children per month who have an active out-of-home placement but no assigned CASA advocate. Closing it requires both advocate recruitment and a stronger court referral pipeline.Track the raw number of unassigned eligible children monthly — not just the percentage — and present it in the board package so the urgency is concrete, not abstract.

Trend — children served growing steadily

Rolling children served grew every month from 126 (Jul 2025) to 160 (Feb 2026) — a 27% increase over 8 months and 80% of the FY26 goal of 200 YTD. The monthly growth rate of roughly 5 additional children per month puts the 200-child annual target within reach if recruitment and referrals maintain current momentum through June.

Financial Sustainability & Community Partnerships — Summary & Insights

This pillar tracks CASA's financial health, revenue diversification, and community partnerships. Total program revenue reached $638K in FY25, growing 64% since FY22 — split between government grants ($499K, 78%) and unrestricted fundraising ($139K, 22%). Total program expenses reached $655K in FY25, producing a -$17K operating deficit after two consecutive surplus years. Personnel costs (salaries + benefits) represent 73% of expenses and grew 67% since FY22. The most important strategic priorities are expanding the unrestricted, donor-controlled revenue base and managing expense growth — particularly personnel and event costs — in proportion to revenue.

Risk — FY25 operating deficit after two surplus years

After posting a $60K surplus in FY23 and an $81K surplus in FY24, CASA ran a -$17K operating deficit in FY25 as expenses ($655K) exceeded revenue ($638K). The deficit reflects investment in program capacity, but with expense growth at 63% since FY22, FY26 budget discipline is essential.Present a scenario analysis of expense growth vs. revenue growth to the board before the FY27 budget is finalized — identify which cost categories are discretionary vs. fixed.

Risk — Toast Gala ROI collapsed from 4.0× to 2.3×

Gala revenue has been virtually flat at $51K–$55K for three years, but event costs grew 86% — from $12,864 to $23,987. ROI fell from 4.0× (FY23) to 3.1× (FY24) to 2.3× (FY25). At this rate, the Gala's net contribution is eroding even as revenue holds steady.Set a per-attendee cost ceiling for FY26 Gala and target returning ROI to ≥3.0× by holding costs flat while growing sponsorship or ticket revenue.

Watch — personnel costs grew 67% in four years

Total personnel expenses (salaries + benefits) grew from $288K in FY22 to $481K in FY25 — a 67% increase. Personnel now represent 73% of all expenses. Payroll salaries alone grew from $329K to $412K between FY24 and FY25 (+25% in one year). This growth is appropriate for a scaling program, but must be tracked against revenue growth to avoid a structural imbalance.Include a personnel-to-revenue ratio in the quarterly financial dashboard — a rising ratio is the earliest indicator of budget stress before it becomes a deficit.

Watch — office and rent costs jumped 69% in three years

Office and rent expenses grew from $22,469 in FY22 to $37,993 in FY25 — a 69% increase. FY24 saw the steepest jump ($22.6K → $32.4K, +43%), likely reflecting a lease renewal or expanded space. At $38K annually, rent is now the fourth-largest expense category, ahead of fundraising costs.Confirm whether the current lease term extends beyond FY27 and model lease renewal risk in the FY28 budget — a market-rate increase on renewal could add $8K–$15K annually to a budget already showing deficit pressure.

Strength — donor growth

Active donors jumped from 108 in FY24 to 278 in FY25 — a 157% increase in one year. This is the most significant growth signal in the dataset and suggests either a successful campaign, expanded outreach, or a major new cultivation effort. Understanding what drove this is critical to repeating it.

Watch — new vs retained

Of FY25's donors, 159 (57%) are brand new — they gave nothing in FY24. Only 71 (26%) were retained from the prior year. First-year donors typically have low retention rates, meaning next year's total could drop sharply without a strong stewardship strategy.Build a personal outreach list of the 71 retained donors now and assign each to a board member or staff relationship before April 30 — set a renewal ask deadline of May 31 so there is time to follow up before the fiscal year closes.

Risk — in-kind concentration and volatility

In-kind giving is structurally concentrated: the top 5 in-kind donors account for approximately 59% of all in-kind revenue in FY25. The FY24 spike to $61.5K was driven by organizational in-kind ($55K from just 16 org donors), which fell to $30K in FY25. A single anchor in-kind donor reducing or ending their gift could materially impact total giving. This is a board-level risk that warrants a diversification strategy.Identify the top 3 in-kind donor relationships and schedule cultivation meetings before Q4; simultaneously, develop a list of 5 prospective in-kind partners to reduce concentration dependency in FY27.

Strength — individual donor surge signals grassroots momentum

Individual donors grew from 77 in FY24 to 199 in FY25 — a 158% increase — contributing $80.9K in cash (75% of total cash giving). This grassroots surge is the most important fundraising signal in the dataset. It suggests either a successful campaign, expanded community presence, or both. Understanding what drove it is critical to repeating it in FY26.Segment the 199 FY25 individual donors by acquisition source (event, referral, digital, direct mail) and identify the top-performing channel — that channel deserves disproportionate investment in FY26.

Strength — cash momentum

Cash giving has grown in 3 of the last 4 fiscal years, from a low of $36K in FY22 to $107K in FY25 — nearly a 3× increase. Individual cash donors drove $80.8K (75%) of the total, showing strong grassroots giving momentum.

Breadth — mid-range donors

The $100–$499 cash gift range has the most donors (97 in FY25), making it the backbone of the donor base. The $1–$99 range had 78 donors. Together these 175 donors make up 76% of cash donor count — significant upgrade potential exists in this group.

Watch — major gift concentration

Just 4 cash donors gave $5,000+ in FY25, yet their gifts totaled $21K — 20% of all cash revenue. Two more in-kind donors gave $5K+ (totaling $16K). This concentration creates real vulnerability. A major gifts cultivation pipeline is worth building now.Develop a prospect list of 5–10 individuals with $5K+ giving capacity for Q4 cultivation and bring it to the next development committee meeting — this is the single highest-leverage financial risk to address before FY27 planning begins.

Strength — FY26 unrestricted goal nearly achieved

As of February 2026, CASA has raised $125,200 in unrestricted funds — 98% of the $128,000 FY26 goal, with four months of fiscal year remaining. This is the strongest current-year signal in the entire dataset: the program is on track to hit its unrestricted fundraising target for the first time against a plan-defined goal, and is well positioned to exceed it if year-end events perform as expected.

Strength — grants pipeline is substantial

CASA submitted $489,311 in total grant applications in FY26, of which $260,500 has been awarded and $228,811 remains pending decision. Even at a conservative 50% success rate on pending grants, the pipeline would add over $114K in new restricted funding — materially strengthening the FY27 budget baseline and reducing reliance on individual donor growth to sustain program capacity.Identify which pending grants have the nearest decision dates and assign a staff lead to follow up with each funder before their deadline — maintaining relationships during review periods improves award rates.

Strength — Toast Gala is the cornerstone unrestricted event

The Toast Gala generated $51,347 (FY23), $54,983 (FY24), and $55,035 (FY25) — three consecutive years with under 4% variance. It is CASA's most reliable single fundraising event and the anchor of unrestricted revenue. At ~$55K/year, it represents 40% of FY25 unrestricted fundraising and has proven remarkably durable. Investing in this event's growth — whether through expanded sponsorship, higher ticket pricing, or increased attendance — is the highest-leverage unrestricted fundraising action available.

Watch — FY25 unrestricted fundraising pulled back $57K from FY24 peak

Total unrestricted fundraising peaked at $196K in FY24 and declined to $139K in FY25 — a $57K drop. Every channel except the Gala contracted: Corporate/Foundation fell from $40K to $16K (−$24K), Individual Donations fell from $35K to $10K (−$26K), and Holiday Appeal fell from $26K to $33K (only the Appeal was slightly up). Only the Gala held steady. Understanding what drove FY24's exceptional performance — a specific campaign, new sponsors, or a major individual gift — is the most important question for FY26 planning.Pull FY24 donor-level data and identify the specific donors and channels behind the $35K individual and $40K corporate spikes — whether those were one-time gifts or repeatable relationships determines the FY26 unrestricted strategy entirely.

Risk — VOCA and NJ State Pass-Thru are 59% of the entire budget

Historical revenue data reveals that VOCA ($147K) and NJ State Pass-Thru ($230K) together represent $377K — 59% of CASA's $638K total program revenue in FY25. NJ State Pass-Thru is the most volatile line in the budget, swinging from $77K (FY22) to $253K (FY24) to $230K (FY25). VOCA is federally appropriated and has faced recurring reduction proposals in Congress. Neither stream is controlled by CASA's development team. A dual reduction scenario — even 15% to each — would create a ~$57K budget gap that no donor campaign could realistically close in one year.Model a 15% and 30% dual reduction scenario in the FY27 budget, identify at least 3 local foundation replacement targets, and ensure the board has seen the full grant concentration picture before approving next year's budget.

Volunteer & Leadership Pipeline — Summary & Insights

This pillar tracks CASA’s volunteer recruitment, retention, and diversity — the foundation of everything the program delivers. Without a sufficient, well-supported, and representative advocate corps, CASA cannot close the gap between eligible children and active cases, maintain cultural continuity with families, or sustain program quality at scale. The active corps has grown from 111 volunteers in FY21 to a peak of 128 in FY24, with FY25 at 121 after approximately 30 departures in FY25 (~23% annual attrition rate). With only 23 new recruits in FY25, the corps netted a loss of 7. Reaching and sustaining the 128 peak requires recruiting roughly 30 advocates per year — a target the program has not yet hit. On the positive side, Black/African American advocates now represent 21%, exceeding the FY26 goal of 20% — the only diversity metric to cross its target. The data below covers headcount trends, demographics, tenure depth, diversity goal tracking, and the equity gap between volunteer composition and the children served.

Strength — diversity trajectory

Non-white volunteers grew from 39% in FY21 to 52% in FY25 — a 13-point shift in four years. White volunteers fell from 61% to 48%, while Asian representation declined slightly from 20% to 19% and Black/African American volunteers grew from 8% to 19%. This trajectory represents meaningful progress toward demographic alignment with the children CASA serves.

Watch — ~23% annual attrition rate

Volunteer count peaked at 128 in FY24 and declined 5.5% to 121 in FY25. With 23 new advocates recruited in FY25 and a net loss of 7, approximately 30 volunteers departed in FY25 — a ~23% annual attrition rate. This means the program must recruit and onboard roughly 30 advocates per year just to hold steady. At the current pace of 23 new recruits, the corps will continue to contract. The 3–7 year tenure band (58 volunteers) is the largest and most experienced segment — protecting this cohort through recognition and role variety is as important as new recruitment.Set a FY26 recruitment target of at least 30 new advocates to offset attrition and return to the FY24 peak, not just 20. Assign at least 2 board members as recruitment ambassadors for information sessions. Track the monthly sworn-in count at every board meeting as a leading indicator.

Strength — volunteer retention

81% of FY25 volunteers have 2+ years of service (98 of 121), and 7 volunteers have 10+ years. This deep bench of experienced advocates is a critical organizational asset — long-tenured volunteers typically carry the most complex cases and provide informal mentorship to newer recruits.

Watch — male representation slipping

The volunteer corps has maintained ~83% female since FY21, consistent with national CASA averages. However, FY26 YTD data shows male representation at 13–15% — a regression from FY25's 17% baseline and below the 17% strategic goal. Without deliberate outreach through fathers' groups, sports coaching networks, and professional associations, this gap will widen.Set a Q3 target of at least 3 new male advocates sworn in before June; brief 2 board members to attend the next information session as male role model ambassadors.

Strength — growing younger cohort

The 21–29 age group grew from 19 volunteers in FY21 to 26 in FY25 — a 37% increase. Younger advocates typically remain active across multiple career transitions, build longer-term relationships with children, and provide the succession pipeline for experienced roles. This trend is one of the most encouraging long-term signals in the volunteer data.

Watch — employment shift affects scheduling

The corps has shifted from a retiree-heavy composition toward full-time working professionals (54 of 121 in FY25, vs. 44 of 111 in FY21). This is good for diversity of professional perspective but means daytime availability for court hearings and training sessions is more constrained. Scheduling flexibility — evening cohort training, virtual onboarding options — should be evaluated against this trend.Survey the current cohort on preferred training times before scheduling the next class — the answer may differ meaningfully from assumptions made when the corps skewed toward retirees.

Watch — Spanish capacity dipped from peak

Spanish language capacity peaked at 20 volunteers in FY24 (14 fluent + 6 conversational), then declined to 18 in FY25 (15 fluent + 3 conversational). Conversational speakers dropped by half — from 6 to 3 — in a single year. With 07008 Carteret now the #1 zip code (30 children, one of the highest Hispanic/Latino concentrations in the county), this trend is moving in the wrong direction. Hindi (10), Urdu (4), and ASL (4) speakers remain stable and provide strong multilingual depth. Spanish recruitment specifically — in Carteret and Perth Amboy — should be a named FY26 priority.Target at least 2 new Spanish-speaking advocate recruits in FY26 through community partnerships in Carteret (07008) and Perth Amboy (08861), where the caseload concentration and language need are highest.

Strength — bilingual goal achieved

As of February 2026, 13% of active advocates are bilingual Spanish speakers — meeting the FY26 13% strategic target. This is one of the few FY26 diversity goals fully achieved. Language-matched advocates improve communication with families, reduce interpreter dependency, and build trust in communities where Spanish is the primary language (notably Carteret and Perth Amboy, which together account for the largest share of the caseload).
