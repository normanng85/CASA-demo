CASA — Child Demographics Dashboard

Child Demographics — Summary & Insights

The active caseload averaged 123 children per month from July 2025 through February 2026, peaking at 128 in August and dipping to 117 in January before recovering. The population is racially diverse, nearly balanced by gender, and skewed toward younger children — with infants and toddlers (0–3) representing the single largest age band and growing steadily throughout the period. Foster care petitions (FN) dominate the case mix but are declining, while guardianship (FG) petitions are on a clear upward trajectory.

Watch — rising disparity

Black/African American children increased from 34 to 42 over 8 months (+24%), now the second-largest racial group at 33% of the census. White representation declined from 31% to 28% over the same period. This growing disparity warrants review of referral and intake patterns.

Trend — infant surge

Infants (0–11 months) rose from 8 to 14 — a 75% increase — the steepest growth of any age band. This signals a need to assess infant-specific placement capacity, foster parent training for young children, and early intervention service availability.

Trend — guardianship growth

FG (guardianship) petitions grew from 14 to 22 (+57%), suggesting more children are moving toward permanent family placements. This is a positive permanency indicator and may reflect successful casework or policy emphasis on kinship care.

Flag — FN petition decline

FN (standard foster care) petitions fell from 89 to 77 (-13%) while total census stayed relatively stable. Monitor whether this reflects genuine permanency progress or a shift in petition classification practices.

Stable — gender balance

The caseload has maintained a near-equal gender split throughout (51% female, 49% male in July; shifting to 46% female, 54% male by February). The gradual male-skew trend is worth tracking across future months.

Stable — Asian representation

Asian children have remained fixed at exactly 8 throughout all 8 months — an unusual pattern that may reflect a data ceiling, classification boundary, or genuine demographic stability worth confirming with intake staff.

CASA — Fundraising Dashboard

Fundraising — Summary & Insights

Fiscal year FY25 (Jul 2024–Jun 2025) was the strongest fundraising year in the 5-year dataset, with $149,253 raised across cash gifts and in-kind contributions — a 15% increase over FY24's $129,526. Cash giving surged 58% to $107K, driven by a dramatic expansion in the donor base from 108 to 278 active donors. However, in-kind giving fell 32% from $61.5K to $42K. The organization is increasingly reliant on individual donors, who contributed 75% of all cash gifts, while organizational in-kind giving remains the single largest in-kind category.

Strength — donor growth

Active donors jumped from 108 in FY24 to 278 in FY25 — a 157% increase in one year. This is the most significant growth signal in the dataset and suggests either a successful campaign, expanded outreach, or a major new cultivation effort. Understanding what drove this is critical to repeating it.

Watch — new vs retained

Of FY25's donors, 159 (57%) are brand new — they gave nothing in FY24. Only 71 (26%) were retained from the prior year. First-year donors typically have low retention rates, meaning next year's total could drop sharply without a strong stewardship strategy.

Risk — in-kind volatility

In-kind gifts swung from $7.7K (FY23) to $61.5K (FY24) to $42K (FY25). The FY24 spike was almost entirely organizational in-kind ($55K from just 16 org donors). That has since fallen to $30K. Without understanding what drove FY24's spike, in-kind projections are unreliable.

Strength — cash momentum

Cash giving has grown in 3 of the last 4 fiscal years, from a low of $36K in FY22 to $107K in FY25 — nearly a 3× increase. Individual cash donors drove $80.8K (75%) of the total, showing strong grassroots giving momentum.

Breadth — mid-range donors

The $100–$499 cash gift range has the most donors (97 in FY25), making it the backbone of the donor base. The $1–$99 range had 78 donors. Together these 175 donors make up 76% of cash donor count — significant upgrade potential exists in this group.

Watch — major gift concentration

Just 4 cash donors gave $5,000+ in FY25, yet their gifts totaled $21K — 20% of all cash revenue. Two more in-kind donors gave $5K+ (totaling $16K). This concentration creates real vulnerability. A major gifts cultivation pipeline is worth building now.

Donor growth rate FY25
+157%vs↓ -1.3% nationally
The Fundraising Effectiveness Project reports the sector saw a 1.3% decline in donor counts in early 2025. CASA's 157% donor surge is a dramatic outlier — far outpacing the national trend and likely driven by a specific campaign or event worth documenting.
New donor retention risk
57% new donorsvs~19% retention nationally
FEP data shows only 19% of first-time donors nationally return to give again. With 159 new donors in FY25, CASA could realistically lose 130+ of them next year without targeted stewardship. This is the single highest-risk metric in the fundraising data.
Cash giving growth
+58% YoYvs+3.6% nationally
The sector raised only 3.6% more in 2024 per FEP benchmarks. CASA's 58% cash growth vastly outperforms this, though some of that growth is likely donor-base expansion rather than average gift size improvement.
Repeat donor reliance
26% retainedvs60%+ of $ from repeat donors
Nationally, repeat donors now account for over 60% of total fundraising dollars (FEP 2025). CASA's 71 retained donors are a valuable core — but at 26% of the donor base they're doing heavy lifting. Growing this group is a top priority.
Small donor ($1–$99) risk
28% of donorsvs↓ -10–12% nationally
FEP reports micro-donors (under $100) declined 10–12% in 2024–2025, a multi-year trend. CASA's 78 donors in this range represent over a quarter of the donor base. Sector-wide, this group is shrinking and their giving raises less per dollar of effort than mid-range donors.
Major gift concentration
20% from 4 donorsvs~50% from top donors nationally
FEP and Giving USA confirm supersize donors ($50K+) contribute roughly half of all nonprofit dollars nationally. CASA's concentration is less extreme — but with just 4 donors contributing 20% of cash, the structural vulnerability is real and mirrors the sector-wide trend.
