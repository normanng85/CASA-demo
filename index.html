Key Signals
Strength — Program Outcomes

62% of FY25 case closures resulted in family reunification, up from 34% in FY24 and the best rate in five years. This is the single strongest program effectiveness signal in the dataset.

Strength — Fundraising Momentum

Active donors jumped 157% (108→278) in FY25, driving a 15% total revenue increase to $149K. Cash giving alone surged 58% to $107K. Understanding what drove this campaign is the top FY26 planning priority.

Strength — Volunteer Diversity

Non-white volunteers grew from 39% (FY21) to 52% (FY25), substantially exceeding the national CASA average of 25–30% and tracking toward demographic alignment with the children CASA serves.

Watch — Volunteer Headcount

Volunteers declined 5.5% from the FY24 peak of 128 to 121 in FY25. New cohort recruitment (23 in FY25) needs to accelerate to offset attrition and return toward the peak.

Watch — Donor Retention Risk

57% of FY25 donors (159 of 278) are giving for the first time. National first-year donor retention is ~19%, meaning ~130 could lapse without active stewardship — the most urgent FY26 fundraising risk.

Risk — Revenue Concentration

4 cash donors gave $5K+ in FY25, representing 20% of all cash revenue ($21K of $107K). Two in-kind donors gave $5K+. This concentration creates real vulnerability if major donors lapse.

Strength — Investment per Child

Cost per child served grew from $293 (FY21) to $894 (FY25) — a 3× increase that reflects the program's fundraising growth outpacing caseload. Greater investment per child typically correlates with more volunteer hours, stronger court advocacy, and better permanency outcomes.

Watch — Geographic Concentration

41% of FY25 cases are concentrated in just 3 zip codes: Carteret (07008, 30 children), Metuchen/Edison (08817, 25), and New Brunswick (08901, 14). Intentional volunteer recruitment in these communities — particularly Spanish-speaking advocates for Carteret and Perth Amboy — would improve geographic matching and cultural continuity for families.

CASA — Child Demographics Dashboard

Child Demographics — Summary & Insights

The active caseload averaged 123 children per month from July 2025 through February 2026, peaking at 128 in August and dipping to 117 in January before recovering. The population is racially diverse, nearly balanced by gender, and skewed toward younger children — with infants and toddlers (0–3) representing the single largest age band and growing steadily throughout the period. Foster care petitions (FN) dominate the case mix but are declining, while guardianship (FG) petitions are on a clear upward trajectory.

Watch — rising disparity

Black/African American children increased from 34 to 42 over 8 months (+24%), now the second-largest racial group at 33% of the census. White representation declined from 31% to 28% over the same period. This growing disparity warrants review of referral and intake patterns.

Trend — infant surge

Infants (0–11 months) rose from 8 to 14 — a 75% increase — the steepest growth of any age band. This signals a need to assess infant-specific placement capacity, foster parent training for young children, and early intervention service availability.

Trend — guardianship growth

FG (guardianship) petitions grew from 14 to 22 (+57%), suggesting more children are moving toward permanent family placements. This is a positive permanency indicator and may reflect successful casework or policy emphasis on kinship care.

Flag — FN petition decline

FN (standard foster care) petitions fell from 89 to 77 (-13%) while total census stayed relatively stable. Monitor whether this reflects genuine permanency progress or a shift in petition classification practices.

Stable — gender balance

The caseload has maintained a near-equal gender split throughout (51% female, 49% male in July; shifting to 46% female, 54% male by February). The gradual male-skew trend is worth tracking across future months.

Stable — Asian representation

Asian children have remained fixed at exactly 8 throughout all 8 months — an unusual pattern that may reflect a data ceiling, classification boundary, or genuine demographic stability worth confirming with intake staff.

CASA — Fundraising Dashboard

Fundraising — Summary & Insights

Fiscal year FY25 (Jul 2024–Jun 2025) was the strongest fundraising year in the 5-year dataset, with $149,253 raised across cash gifts and in-kind contributions — a 15% increase over FY24's $129,526. Cash giving surged 58% to $107K, driven by a dramatic expansion in the donor base from 108 to 278 active donors. However, in-kind giving fell 32% from $61.5K to $42K. The organization is increasingly reliant on individual donors, who contributed 75% of all cash gifts, while organizational in-kind giving remains the single largest in-kind category.

Strength — donor growth

Active donors jumped from 108 in FY24 to 278 in FY25 — a 157% increase in one year. This is the most significant growth signal in the dataset and suggests either a successful campaign, expanded outreach, or a major new cultivation effort. Understanding what drove this is critical to repeating it.

Watch — new vs retained

Of FY25's donors, 159 (57%) are brand new — they gave nothing in FY24. Only 71 (26%) were retained from the prior year. First-year donors typically have low retention rates, meaning next year's total could drop sharply without a strong stewardship strategy.

Risk — in-kind volatility

In-kind gifts swung from $7.7K (FY23) to $61.5K (FY24) to $42K (FY25). The FY24 spike was almost entirely organizational in-kind ($55K from just 16 org donors). That has since fallen to $30K. Without understanding what drove FY24's spike, in-kind projections are unreliable.

Strength — cash momentum

Cash giving has grown in 3 of the last 4 fiscal years, from a low of $36K in FY22 to $107K in FY25 — nearly a 3× increase. Individual cash donors drove $80.8K (75%) of the total, showing strong grassroots giving momentum.

Breadth — mid-range donors

The $100–$499 cash gift range has the most donors (97 in FY25), making it the backbone of the donor base. The $1–$99 range had 78 donors. Together these 175 donors make up 76% of cash donor count — significant upgrade potential exists in this group.

Watch — major gift concentration

Just 4 cash donors gave $5,000+ in FY25, yet their gifts totaled $21K — 20% of all cash revenue. Two more in-kind donors gave $5K+ (totaling $16K). This concentration creates real vulnerability. A major gifts cultivation pipeline is worth building now.

Donor growth rate FY25
+157%vs↓ -1.3% nationally
The Fundraising Effectiveness Project reports the sector saw a 1.3% decline in donor counts in early 2025. CASA's 157% donor surge is a dramatic outlier — far outpacing the national trend and likely driven by a specific campaign or event worth documenting.
New donor retention risk
57% new donorsvs~19% retention nationally
FEP data shows only 19% of first-time donors nationally return to give again. With 159 new donors in FY25, CASA could realistically lose 130+ of them next year without targeted stewardship. This is the single highest-risk metric in the fundraising data.
Cash giving growth
+58% YoYvs+3.6% nationally
The sector raised only 3.6% more in 2024 per FEP benchmarks. CASA's 58% cash growth vastly outperforms this, though some of that growth is likely donor-base expansion rather than average gift size improvement.
Repeat donor reliance
26% retainedvs60%+ of $ from repeat donors
Nationally, repeat donors now account for over 60% of total fundraising dollars (FEP 2025). CASA's 71 retained donors are a valuable core — but at 26% of the donor base they're doing heavy lifting. Growing this group is a top priority.
Small donor ($1–$99) risk
28% of donorsvs↓ -10–12% nationally
FEP reports micro-donors (under $100) declined 10–12% in 2024–2025, a multi-year trend. CASA's 78 donors in this range represent over a quarter of the donor base. Sector-wide, this group is shrinking and their giving raises less per dollar of effort than mid-range donors.
Major gift concentration
20% from 4 donorsvs~50% from top donors nationally
FEP and Giving USA confirm supersize donors ($50K+) contribute roughly half of all nonprofit dollars nationally. CASA's concentration is less extreme — but with just 4 donors contributing 20% of cash, the structural vulnerability is real and mirrors the sector-wide trend.

CASA — Volunteer Demographics Dashboard

Volunteer Demographics — Summary & Insights

CASA's active volunteer corps has grown from 111 volunteers in FY21 to a peak of 128 in FY24, with FY25 at 121 reflecting natural attrition and turnover. The corps is overwhelmingly female (~83%), highly educated (Bachelors or higher: ~76% in FY25), and meaningfully multilingual — with Hindi, Spanish, and Urdu speakers well-represented. Volunteer tenure has deepened year over year, with more advocates now in their 3–7-year range.

Strength — diversity trajectory

Non-white volunteers grew from 39% in FY21 to 52% in FY25 — a 13-point shift in four years. White volunteers fell from 61% to 48%, while Asian representation grew from 20% to 19% and Black/African American volunteers grew from 8% to 19%. This trajectory represents meaningful progress toward demographic alignment with the children CASA serves.

Watch — headcount plateau

Volunteer count peaked at 128 in FY24 and declined 5.5% to 121 in FY25. New cohort recruitment (0–1 year volunteers: 23 in FY25) needs to offset annual attrition to avoid a sustained downward trend. The 3–7 year tenure band (58 volunteers) is the largest segment and represents the core experienced corps.

Strength — volunteer retention

81% of FY25 volunteers have 2+ years of service (98 of 121), and 7 volunteers have 10+ years. This deep bench of experienced advocates is a critical organizational asset — long-tenured volunteers typically carry the most complex cases and provide informal mentorship to newer recruits.

Stable — gender composition

The volunteer corps has maintained ~83% female since FY21, consistent with national CASA averages. While gender diversity is limited, this stability suggests reliable recruitment pipelines. The 21 male volunteers (17%) are slightly above the national CASA average of ~14% male.

Strength — language assets

18 Spanish speakers, 10 Hindi speakers, 4 Urdu speakers, and 4 ASL users provide direct language access for a significant share of the caseload. With 17–18% of active children identified as Hispanic/Latino, Spanish language capacity among volunteers is particularly valuable for family communication and court advocacy.

Context — vs. National CASA averages

Nationally, CASA programs average ~84% female volunteers and ~16% male — this program's 83%/17% split is closely aligned. Non-white volunteer representation nationally is approximately 25–30% and growing; at 52% non-white in FY25, this program substantially exceeds the national average, reflecting intentional diversity recruitment. Nationally, ~65–70% of CASA volunteers hold a bachelor's degree or higher; this program's ~76% with Bachelors or higher in FY25 is above average. The national volunteer-to-child ratio averages roughly 1:1 to 1:2; this program's ratio supports its full caseload. (Source: National CASA/GAL Association annual data.)

CASA — Removal Zip Code Dashboard

Active Children — Removal Zip Codes

Each row in the source data represents one child assigned to CASA, identified by removal zip code, case number, and original assigned-to-CASA date. Duplicate case numbers reflect multiple children on the same case — this is expected and correct. Removals are geographically concentrated in a Middlesex County corridor: Carteret (07008), New Brunswick (08901), and Perth Amboy (08861) are consistently the highest-volume zip codes, together representing roughly one-third of all cases each year. Total verified cases: FY21 = 168 · FY22 = 200 · FY23 = 203 · FY24 = 191 · FY25 = 167.

Watch — geographic concentration

The top 3 zip codes (07008 Carteret, 08901 New Brunswick, 08861 Perth Amboy) consistently account for 30–37% of all removals each year. Over five years, 07008 generated 130 child assignments and 08901 generated 115 — the two highest-volume zips by a wide margin. These are dense urban communities with elevated rates of family system involvement.

Watch — 08817 rising trend

Metuchen/Edison (08817) shows the sharpest growth trajectory in the dataset: 8 cases in FY21, steady through FY23 (11), then accelerating to 18 in FY24 and 25 in FY25 — a 3× increase over five years. By FY25 it is the second-highest zip code. This emerging concentration warrants attention for volunteer placement and recruitment in the Edison area.

Opportunity — targeted recruitment

Volunteer recruitment in the 07008 (Carteret), 08901 (New Brunswick), 08817 (Metuchen/Edison), and 08861 (Perth Amboy) corridors could improve geographic match between advocates and families. Spanish-speaking volunteers are especially valuable in Carteret and Perth Amboy, both of which have large Hispanic/Latino populations.

Context — 40+ zip codes served

Over the five-year period, more than 40 distinct zip codes appear across all years, spanning 07xxx (northern Middlesex/Union County) and 08xxx (central/southern Middlesex) ranges. The caseload peaked at 203 children in FY23 and has since declined to 167 in FY25 — a 18% reduction likely reflecting both systemic trends and natural case closure patterns.

What this tells us: The average age of children in CASA care is remarkably stable at 8.7–8.9 years. The 0–3 age band (infants and toddlers) is consistently the program's largest group, representing roughly 27% of the caseload each month. The near-absence of variation across months means the program's age profile is structurally determined — driven by systemic referral patterns rather than seasonal intake fluctuations. This consistency simplifies resource planning but underscores the sustained need for volunteers comfortable working with very young children and their families.
